        *   **Part 1 (Eliminating Absurdities):** Removes states that are primitively absurd (e.g., `{p, pÃÖ}`). This reflects reasoners' ability to recognize and discard contradictions, though not automatically.
        *   **Part 2 (Division):** Uses a `Division` operation to remove an issue (`Œî`) that is "wholly contained" within another view (`Œì`). This allows for the derivation of "reduced" canonical conclusions (e.g., from "Q and J but not A" to simply "Q and J"), reflecting the cognitive aim to simplify issues.

5.  **Reasoning with Suppositions:**
    *   **Suppose Operation (`À¢`):** Formalizes the strategic use of suppositional reasoning, where a view is updated to incorporate a temporary assumption, affecting both its main alternatives and its supposition.
    *   **Depose Operation (`·¥∞`):** The "return ticket" for suppositions. It undoes a supposition, integrating the conclusions derived under the assumption back into a view with a trivial supposition.
    *   **Strategic Advantage:** Suppositional reasoning is shown to strategically simplify complex problems by temporarily narrowing the scope of inquiry, making inferences more manageable.

6.  **Ex Falso Quodlibet:** The theory addresses the classical principle that anything can be derived from a contradiction. Koralus explains that while logically valid, this inference is "naively repugnant" because it goes directly against the erotetic aim of reducing the issue set and moving towards answers.

### Section 8

Part 8 significantly advances the Erotetic Theory of Reason by introducing mechanisms for strategic inquiry and formally defining different standards of rational inference.

**Key Points:**

1.  **Inquire (I) and Query (Q) Operations:**
    *   **Inquire (`I`):** Formalizes the ability to raise "polar questions" (yes/no issues) on any propositional atom without making factual assumptions. This operation is effortful, as it tends to multiply alternatives, but is crucial for making tacit alternatives explicit.
        *   It explains how **repugnant validities** (e.g., inferring "A or B" from "A") can be derived, even if counterintuitive, by explicitly raising the possibility of 'B'.
        *   Crucially, `Inquire` can **block illusory inferences** by forcing consideration of alternatives that would otherwise be neglected, demonstrating that the `Answer` procedure itself is not inherently fallacious.
        *   Systematic use of `Inquire` allows for the characterization of classical reasoning.
    *   **Query (`Q`):** An operation used to determine if a suppositional conclusion follows from a view. It checks if alternatives in the conclusion are sufficiently "contained" within the premise view given the supposition. It can lead to fallacies if not enough `Inquire` has been applied to fully explore alternatives.

2.  **Default Inference Procedure and Core Definitions:**
    *   Koralus outlines a **Default Inference Procedure** that combines the various view operations (Product, Sum, Factor, Answer, Merge, Inquire, Query) to model how reasoners process sequences of premises for "what follows?" or "does X follow?" tasks.
    *   **Commit (Def 2.27)** and **Reorient (Def 2.28)** operations are introduced to manage the reasoner's stock of accepted views and their current focus.
    *   **Inference (Def 2.29)** is formally defined as a finite sequence of these inference steps within an erotetic state.

3.  **Erotetic Equilibrium ‚Äì The Standard for Correct Reasoning:**
    *   **Erotetic Equilibrium of a View (Def 2.30):** A view `G` is in equilibrium with respect to a set of views `Œ£` if inquiring on any view `D` in `Œ£` makes no difference to `G`. This signifies that all relevant questions have been considered.
    *   **Absolute Erotetic Equilibrium of an Inference (Def 2.32):** An inference is in absolute equilibrium if there exists an alternative, "manifest" inference where, for every application of the `Answer` operation, the views involved are in erotetic equilibrium with respect to the atoms in the answer.
    *   **Guaranteed Classical Validity:** A major achievement: Absolute Erotetic Equilibrium *guarantees classical soundness and completeness* for propositional reasoning (Theorems 2.1 & 2.2), linking the Erotetic Theory directly to classical logic under ideal conditions. This reflects a "Socratic" ideal of rationality where conclusions withstand "hostile questioning."

4.  **Restricted Erotetic Equilibrium ‚Äì Explaining Everyday Rationality:**
    *   Koralus argues that absolute equilibrium is too demanding for *all* human reasoning, as people often make rational inferences without considering every possible alternative.
    *   **Topic of Inquiry (TI) (Def 2.33):** Introduced as a set of states circumscribing what is at issue in a specific inquiry.
    *   **Restricted Erotetic Equilibrium (Def 2.34):** An inference is in restricted equilibrium if, for every `Answer` step, only those alternatives within the views that fit the defined `Topic of Inquiry` need to be in erotetic equilibrium.
    *   **Explaining Rational Fallacies:** This concept explains cases like the "tiger inference" (e.g., seeing orange fur and concluding "tiger" when being hunted) where a classically invalid inference is deemed perfectly rational *given the context* and the specific topic of inquiry (e.g., "tiger cases"). It doesn't assume the conclusion, but means the reasoner isn't "on the hook" for considering alternatives outside the topic, unless directly forced.

5.  **Norms of Rationality:**
    *   **Equilibrium Requirement (Def 2.35):** If a judgment isn't in restricted equilibrium for a `TI` a reasonable person would adopt, the reasoner is not fully rational.
    *   **Equilibrium Guarantee (Def 2.36):** The more controversial claim that if a judgment *is* in restricted equilibrium for a `TI` a reasonable person would adopt, the reasoner *is* fully rational. This would require an account of what constitutes a "reasonable topic of inquiry."
    *   The theory emphasizes that for realistic rational norms, **restricted erotetic equilibrium** is more plausible than absolute equilibrium, allowing for different standards of rationality based on the context and purpose of the inquiry.

It also addresses **antecedent strengthening** by linking it to the `Topic of Inquiry` and restricted equilibrium.

6.  **Selection Tasks and Information Seeking:** Koralus proposes that Wason's card selection task errors stem from the general difficulty of contradiction detection and general problem-solving strategies, not unique mechanisms. This section of Koralus's 'Reason and Inquiry' extends the Erotetic Theory's analysis of conditional reasoning, explaining a wide range of empirical findings and setting the stage for predicate logic.

### Section 19

This section, "Decision and Practical Reasoning," extends the Erotetic Theory of Inquiry (ETR) to human decision-making, aiming to explain both its successes and systematic failures. It argues that decisions, like inferences, involve asking a question and seeking an answer, with failures often stemming from a lack of "erotetic equilibrium."

Key points include:

1.  **The Problem of Success and Failure:** Humans are capable of highly effective decision-making, yet also exhibit systematic breakdowns of control even when basic conditions (information, time, etc.) are available. The ETR focuses on failures inherent in the process of practical reasoning itself, rather than external factors.

2.  **The "Narrow Frame" Problem & Too Few Alternatives:**
    *   A prominent failure is the tendency to consider too few alternative possibilities. Studies show "whether-or-not" decisions (e.g., buy or not buy) have significantly higher failure rates and lower quality ratings than multi-alternative decisions (e.g., Nutt 1993, Gem√ºnden & Hauschild 1985).
    *   **Opportunity Cost Neglect (Example 90):** Explicitly framing an alternative as "keep the money for other purchases" significantly changes decisions compared to a simple "not buy" option, even though objectively the situation is the same (Frederick et al. 2009). This fragility suggests a decision is not in erotetic equilibrium if merely drawing attention to known options alters the outcome.
    *   **Caveat:** Simply adding *more* alternatives isn't always beneficial; too many similar options can overwhelm decision-makers (Iyengar & Lepper 2000), and the **Decoy Effect (Example 91)** shows that a "dominated" option (one clearly inferior to another) can manipulate choices towards a more expensive alternative, without anyone actually choosing the decoy (Ariely 2008). The ETR interprets this as "jumping to conclusions" by treating a partial answer as a full one.

3.  **Framing Effects on Choice and Judgment:**
    *   **Choosing vs. Rejecting (Example 92):** In a hypothetical child custody case, Parent B, who has both more positive and more negative traits, is chosen by the majority both for *awarding* and *denying* custody (Shafir 1993). This is because decision-makers focus on reasons for choosing when awarding, and reasons for rejecting when denying, leading to inconsistent choices in objectively equivalent situations.
    *   Similar effects are observed in moral judgments about "obligatory" vs. "impermissible" acts (Koralus & Alfano 2017).
    *   The ETR argues this reflects an irrational application of *stable priorities* rather than fundamental preference changes, preserving the idea of rational control.
    *   **Asian Disease Problem (Example 93):** Framing identical outcomes in terms of "people die" vs. "people saved" significantly alters preferred treatments (Tversky & Kahneman 1981).

4.  **The Endowment Effect (Example 94):**
    *   People tend to prefer keeping an item they were randomly given over trading it for an objectively equivalent alternative (Kahneman, Knetsch, & Thaler 1990).
    *   The ETR critiques explanations based on fundamental **loss aversion**, arguing this implies intrinsic irrationality, which undermines the concept of decision-making control.
    *   **Empirical Counter-evidence:** The endowment effect is diminished or absent in "brokers" who don't own the item themselves (Morewedge et al. 2009) and, crucially, in experienced traders in real-world markets; it disappears with experience (List 2003, 2004). This suggests it's not a deeply ingrained, immutable irrationality.

5.  **Erotetic Theory of Decision-Making as a Solution:**
    *   The ETR proposes that decision-making involves: (1) raising a decision question, (2) considering views of consequences of alternatives, and (3) treating priorities as answers.
    *   Systematic failures arise from "jumping to conclusions" or accepting "illusory reasons" due to a lack of erotetic equilibrium, mirroring the explanation for fallacies in inferential reasoning.
    *   By *not* jumping to conclusions and moving towards erotetic equilibrium, decision-makers can make choices that align with classical rational choice theory.
    *   The ETR aims to bridge classical economics (viewed as studying decision-making *under erotetic equilibrium*) and behavioral economics (explaining deviations), offering a unified account where both rational success and systematic failure flow from the same fundamental principles.

### Section 20

This section, "Part 20: Practical Reasoning," extends the Erotetic Theory of Reason (ETR) to decision-making, presenting a dynamic, question-based account that diverges from classical rational choice theory and reframes the Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) model.

Here's a breakdown of the key points:

1.  **Critique of Classical Decision Theory:**
    *   Classical rational decision-making is impractical: it demands considering *all* alternative actions and their complete expected consequences "all at once," leaving no room for dynamic planning or deliberation (the "frame problem").
    *   The ETR aligns with Harman and Bratman's view that practical reasoning is a **dynamic process of revising intentions** (filtering or broadening prior intentions), rather than static selection from an exhaustive set.

2.  **Erotetic Reframing of BDI Components:**
    *   Koralus's ETR replaces the primitive notions of intention, belief, and desire with erotetic (question-based) counterparts, offering a more technical and flexible foundation.

3.  **Intentions ‚Üí Decision Questions & Doings:**
    *   The ordinary concept of "intention" is deemed ambiguous (e.g., Knobe effect).
    *   The ETR introduces **"doings" (`do(A)`)** as technical instructions that various performance systems (motor or mental, like generating further inquiries) can carry out, with any state as a potential goal.
    *   **Pure Decision Questions (DQ)** are sets of alternative doings. These can be "open" (including the option `0`, or "do nothing") or "closed."
    *   The theory explains the **dynamic broadening of a prior decision question** by inquiring on additional doings, effectively expanding the set of alternatives being considered (e.g., from `{do(go north), 0}` to `{do(go north), do(go south), 0}`).

4.  **Beliefs ‚Üí Direct Consequence Views (DCV):**
    *   Instead of complex "beliefs" about all possible outcomes, the ETR focuses on **"direct consequence views" (DCV)**. These are finite, often small sets of views about the immediate, salient consequences of specific doings.
    *   This approach addresses the "frame problem" by limiting the scope of consideration to what is *directly* relevant or has been dynamically inferred. DCVs can incorporate EAP weights (probabilities), as discussed in Chapter 5.

5.  **Desires ‚Üí Priority Views:**
    *   The ETR critiques primitive "desires" for being dynamic, question-relative, and often systematic rather than "punctate" (isolated).
    *   It introduces **"priority views" (PR)**, which function as "pro tanto reasons." These are structured to update EAP weights based on the separable positive or negative bearing of features in a situation (e.g., "chocolate flavor" adding positive weight, "dairy content" adding negative).

6.  **Integrating Probabilities and Priorities with Double-Weights:**
    *   To accommodate both **multiplicative combination for uncertainty/probability** (from Chapter 5) and **additive combination for priorities**, the system introduces **"double-weights" (`mÀ£.m'‚Å∫`)**.
    *   The definitions of Equilibrium Answer Potential (EAP), Sum, and Product of views are extended to handle these double-weights, ensuring that probabilities combine multiplicatively and priorities combine additively.

7.  **Default Decision Procedure:**
    *   The **"default decision"** process for an erotetic agent involves:
        1.  Taking a decision question (DQ).
        2.  Updating it with relevant direct consequence views (CV).
        3.  Further updating it with applicable priority views (PR).
    *   The final decision favors the alternative doings that accumulate the highest combined "answer weight" (reflecting both their likely consequences and their alignment with priorities). This process acts as a "filtering" mechanism, similar to how BDI models revise intentions. The section notes that "attention" will play a crucial role in guiding issue structures for practical reasoning.

This section details how the Erotetic Theory of Inquiry (ETR) explains various systematic failures in decision-making, attributing them to different forms of "erotetic disequilibrium," and then connects the ETR to classical expected utility maximization under conditions of equilibrium, finally touching on the origins of decision questions.

8.  **Affordances and Action-Centered Priorities:** Addressing the question "where do questions come from?", the section posits that substantive questions (and thus `do` atoms, or potential actions) can arise *outside* explicit reasoning. These action-oriented perceptions can bypass full practical reasoning, providing a fundamental, non-inferential source for the alternatives considered in decision questions.
